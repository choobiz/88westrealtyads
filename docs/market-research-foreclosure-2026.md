## Executive summary

1. **Active inventory is real and substantial.** As of 2026-05-01, BC has **389 active foreclosure / court-ordered listings** on aggregator realestatecoalharbour.com (33 pages × 12 per page), and KeyHomes shows 100+ active court-ordered listings province-wide. Multiple Greater Vancouver / Fraser Valley examples in the East Vancouver, Mission, Langley, Kamloops, and Mackenzie regions. The supply is not theoretical.
2. **The mortgage-renewal-cliff narrative is the single biggest tailwind.** CMHC: ~2 million Canadian mortgages renew across 2025–2026 (1.2M in 2025 + 1.15M in 2026). Pandemic-era mortgages at <2% renew at 4–5%. CMHC explicitly notes that **BC and Ontario have rising delinquency rates** as renewals begin. This is the macro engine behind the supply ramp.
3. **BC's process is judicial — different from Ontario.** Every sale must be approved by the BC Supreme Court. Sales are "as is, where is" with no warranties. Buyers must accept Schedule A. Court date risk: a competing buyer can outbid the original accepted offer in open court. Possession risk: former owner/tenants may still be in the property.
4. **The lead magnet is defensible.** Court-ordered listings flow through MLS + Supreme Court of BC + realtor channels. Multiple competitors already aggregate (rodfriesen.com, reimers.ca, bchb.ca, keyhomes.ca, realestatecoalharbour.com) — but most are ugly IDX template dumps. A real "Daily Updated" pipeline is operationally feasible.
5. **The owner-occupier path is genuinely real**, contrary to the "investors only" cliché. Many BC court-ordered listings are condo/townhouse stock at sub-$500K prices ($259K Mackenzie, $459K Langley) — owner-occupier territory. The bigger blocker for owner-occupiers is process anxiety, not affordability.

## 2. Mortgage renewal cliff

### The numbers

- **CMHC (Nov 2024):** ~2 million Canadian mortgages renew across 2025–2026, costing borrowers ~$15B/year in additional payments. Toronto identified as the "epicentre."
- **Oxford Economics (Davenport):** 1.2 million mortgages renew in 2025 — **17% of total outstanding mortgages, worth $350B**.
- **nesto / 2026:** 1.15 million mortgages renewing in 2026.
- **Pandemic-rate baseline:** Borrowers who locked in at <2% during 2020–2021 are renewing into 4–5% range as of 2026.
- **Variable-rate exposure:** A 5-year variable mortgage renewing October 2026 could see payments jump 76% if mortgage rates stay near 6%. Even with aggressive Bank of Canada cuts, variable-rate payments would still be up ~20% (TD Economics).

### The counter-narrative (be aware)

TD Bank's December 2024 update: faster Bank of Canada rate cuts + looser federal mortgage lending guidelines mean the renewal shock "is not as worrisome." Many borrowers shifted to short-term mortgages anticipating rate drops, so by renewal they're in a softer rate environment than feared. This is a published view; it doesn't negate the supply pressure but tempers the doomsday narrative.

**Landing-page implication:** the "supply is coming" hook works without needing a doom narrative. We're not predicting a crash — we're saying *the inventory exists right now*, and our job is to surface it for buyers ready to act.

## 3. BC court-ordered sale process

### Mechanics

BC uses a **judicial sale process** governed by the **Supreme Court of British Columbia**. Every step is court-approved. Different from Ontario's power-of-sale, which is contractual and runs without court oversight.

**Timeline overview** (compiled from carlycarey.com, uniqueproperties.ca, pazder via LinkedIn):

1. Homeowner misses payments → lender files for foreclosure
2. Court orders the property listed for sale (the "Order for Conduct of Sale")
3. Property is listed with a regular MLS REALTOR®
4. Buyer makes offer to lender (via lender's solicitor)
5. Conditions (subjects) are typically removed BEFORE court approval
6. Lender's lawyer applies to BC Supreme Court for sale approval
7. Court hearing date is scheduled (often 2–6 weeks after subject-removal)
8. **At the court date, OTHER buyers can show up with competing subject-free offers.** The judge reviews all offers; usually awards to highest bidder. If prices are close, time-of-first-offer can break ties.
9. Once court approves, completion happens typically **1 to 10 days later** (lender's lawyer can extend up to ~10 more days without re-approval).

### Key buyer-side rules

- **"As is, where is."** No warranties, no disclosures. Buyer assumes 100% of property condition risk.
- **Schedule A required** with offer — releases owner/lender from any condition responsibility. Holes in walls? Buyer's problem.
- **Subjects acceptable but no contingency safety.** Buyer can request inspection during the offer window, but once subject-free in court, no walking back.
- **Appliances are NOT included.** They can vanish before possession; treat any appliances at possession as a bonus.
- **Possession risk.** Court approval transfers title but the former owner or tenants may still occupy the property. Buyer may need to pursue legal eviction. This is *the most under-discussed* risk in foreclosure marketing.

### BC vs. Ontario in one line

In Ontario, the lender controls the sale ("power of sale"). In BC, the *court* controls the sale (judicial sale). **BC buyers can be outbid in court** even after weeks of work; Ontario buyers cannot. This is a meaningful storytelling distinction for the LP.

## 4. Audience hooks

### Investors

Vancouver foreclosure investors split across three archetypes:

- **Cash flippers** — looking for ~25–40% under-list to renovate and resell. Want volume in the deal pipeline; will tolerate possession friction.
- **BRRRR holders** — Buy / Renovate / Rent / Refinance / Repeat. Need acquisition price low enough that post-rehab appraisal supports cash-out refi. Currently constrained by 2025 lending tightness.
- **Long-hold landlords** — willing to take possession-risk + tenant-eviction overhead in exchange for sub-market entry price.

**What they need from a landing page:**
- Volume + cadence proof ("47 active listings this week" or similar)
- ROI math primer (back-of-envelope worksheet)
- Court-date calendar / showing schedule
- Direct line to a buyer-agent who has actually closed BC court-ordered transactions

**Decision timeline:** 24–72 hours from list discovery to written offer. Speed beats polish in this segment.

### Owner-occupiers / first-time buyers

The realistic owner-occupier opportunity in BC court-ordered sales is the sub-$600K condo and townhouse stock (Langley $459K, Mackenzie $259K, etc.). The "save 20–40%" claim is more myth than reality at this tier — court-ordered sales typically clear within 5–15% of fair market value because BC's judicial process exposes the property to multiple competing bids.

**Real benefits owner-occupiers can capture:**
- Access to inventory other regular MLS buyers ignore (because of the "weird process" perception)
- Lower competition from emotional bidders during the subject-removal phase
- A slightly lower price-per-square-foot in a tight market

**The fears that block them:**
- "Is this a scam? Why is it cheap?"
- "Can I even get a mortgage on a court-ordered sale?" (Yes, but slightly tighter underwriting — major banks are fine; some B-lenders refuse)
- "What if the previous owner won't leave?" (Real risk; 88West must explain how this is handled)
- "What if I get outbid in court after I've spent on inspections and lawyers?" (Real risk; should be disclosed)

**Decision timeline:** weeks to months. They want education + handholding, not just deal flow.

### One-page-serves-both?

Yes, with care. Hero + above-fold needs to land both audiences (the lead magnet does this naturally — "free list" appeals to both). Below the fold, separate "For investors" and "For first-time buyers" sub-sections with audience-specific content. Final CTA can route both to the same form (capture audience type as a field).

## 5. Lead-magnet defensibility

**"Free Foreclosure List Updated Daily" is operationally credible.**

Source pipeline candidates:
- **MLS / REBGV feed** with court-ordered tag (most active foreclosures are MLS-listed; standard realtor data feed)
- **Supreme Court of BC** electronic filing system (SCBC eSearch) for new "Order for Conduct of Sale" filings
- **BC Court Bailiff** notices (public)
- **Paid feeds** (ListShield, distressed-tag MLS aggregators) — costs ~$50–100/month
- **Manual analyst** to filter MLS for "Order for Conduct of Sale" / "Foreclosure" / "Court-Ordered" keywords in remarks (current state of the art for most aggregator competitors)

The right combination depends on 88West's appetite for paid data. Minimum-viable is a daily MLS-keyword filter (manual or scripted) — every competitor in this research is using essentially that approach.

**Warning:** the page must NOT promise data 88West can't deliver. If "daily" turns into "weekly when I get to it," the credibility collapses fast. Better to under-promise: "Updated every business morning."
